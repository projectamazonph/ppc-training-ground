# Bid Elevator Prep: Ready to Bid

You've learned about bid strategies and placement adjustments. Now it's time to put that knowledge into action. The **Bid Elevator** simulation will test your ability to make smart bidding decisions under realistic conditions — one scenario at a time, each with different products, margins, and market dynamics.

> 🎯 **Analogy**: The Bid Elevator is like an elevator pitch in reverse. Instead of pitching your product, you're pitching your bid — and the "jury" is Amazon's auction algorithm. Get the number right and you win. Overshoot and you lose money. Undershoot and you lose the auction entirely.

## What the Bid Elevator Tests

The simulation evaluates you across three dimensions:

**Accuracy** — How close are your bids to the optimal number? You don't need to hit it exactly. The scoring engine gives partial credit based on proximity. A bid that's 10% off scores much higher than one that's 50% off.

**Reasoning consistency** — Do your bids match the scenario's constraints? If margins are tight and the target ACoS is 15%, your bids should be lower than a scenario with 40% margins and 25% ACoS.

**Strategic awareness** — Do you adjust based on competitive signals? If a scenario mentions aggressive competition, you need to decide whether to compete or pivot.

> 💡 **Tip**: The scoring engine isn't looking for one "right" number. It's looking for a bid that reflects good judgment — a number you could defend to a client.

## The Core Bidding Formula

Every bid you place should trace back to this:

**Max CPC = Price × CVR × Target ACoS**

**Example:**
- Product price: $29.99
- Expected CVR: 12%
- Target ACoS: 25%

Max CPC = $29.99 × 0.12 × 0.25 = **$0.90**

You should never bid above $0.90 for this product if your ACoS target is 25%. Any CPC above this pushes ACoS above target.

> 📌 **Key Takeaway**: This formula is your hard ceiling. Your actual bid should be at or below it — adjusted for competition, placement strategy, and keyword confidence.

**But should you bid exactly $0.90?** Not necessarily. Your actual bid should factor in:
- **Market CPC** — If the market average is $0.50, there's no reason to bid $0.90
- **Placement strategy** — If you want top-of-search, you may need to bid closer to your ceiling
- **Confidence level** — New keywords with uncertain CVR deserve lower bids than proven performers

## Reading the Scenario Brief

Each scenario gives you critical information. Extract these variables before you bid:

**Product economics:**
- Selling price
- Product margin (or cost to calculate it)
- Target ACoS

**Market data:**
- Estimated CPC range for the keyword
- Competition level (low, medium, high)
- Search volume indicator

**Strategic context:**
- Is this a launch (awareness priority) or established product (profit priority)?
- Is the keyword brand, product-specific, or category-level?
- Any seasonal or competitive dynamics?

> 💡 **Tip**: Don't rush past the scenario brief. Read every line. The brief contains the numbers you need for the formula — skip it and you're guessing.

## The Bidding Decision Tree

For each scenario, follow this flow:

**Step 1: Calculate your max CPC.** This is your hard ceiling. Write it down.

**Step 2: Check the market CPC range.**
- If market CPC is **above** your max CPC → you have a problem (see options below)
- If market CPC is **below** your max CPC → proceed to Step 3

**Step 3: Set your bid based on intent level:**
- **High intent** (brand terms, exact product name) → Bid near max CPC
- **Medium intent** (category terms, use-case phrases) → Bid at 60–80% of max CPC
- **Low intent** (broad discovery) → Bid at 30–50% of max CPC

**Step 4: Adjust for strategy:**
- **Launch mode** → Bid 20–30% above normal to accelerate visibility
- **Profit mode** → Bid at or below calculated max CPC
- **Defense mode** → Bid aggressively on own brand terms regardless of ACoS

> ⚠️ **Watch out**: If the market CPC is above your max CPC, you can't profitably bid on that keyword at your target ACoS. Your options are: accept a higher ACoS, improve your CVR through listing optimization, or walk away and find less competitive terms.

## Common Bidding Mistakes

**Bidding without calculating max CPC.** If you don't know your ceiling, you're guessing. Always do the math first.

**Using the same bid for every scenario.** A $30 product with 10% CVR and a $15 product with 20% CVR have very different max CPCs. Adjust per scenario.

**Bidding above max CPC because "competition is high."** If you can't afford to win profitably, don't win. A sale at a loss is worse than no sale.

**Ignoring bid strategy context.** The scenario may specify which strategy is in use. Fixed bids = your bid is your CPC ceiling. Dynamic Up and Down = your actual CPC could be 2x your bid.

**Overbidding on broad terms.** Broad match keywords should always have the lowest bids. They're research tools, not profit drivers.

## Try This: Practice Calculation

Before entering the simulation, try this:

**Scenario**: You sell a premium coffee grinder at $49.99. Cost is $22.00. Target ACoS is 20%. The keyword "burr coffee grinder" has 8% CVR and market CPC range of $1.20–$2.50.

**Step 1**: Max CPC = $49.99 × 0.08 × 0.20 = **$0.80**

**Step 2**: Market CPC ($1.20–$2.50) is ABOVE your max CPC ($0.80). You can't profitably bid on this keyword at 20% ACoS.

**Your options:**
- Accept 30% ACoS → Max CPC = $1.20 (bottom of market range)
- Improve CVR to 12% → Max CPC = $1.20 at 20% ACoS
- Walk away and focus on less expensive terms

> 📌 **Key Takeaway**: The Bid Elevator isn't just about "what number do I type?" It's about "should I even be bidding here?" Good bidding is as much about walking away as it is about choosing a number.
