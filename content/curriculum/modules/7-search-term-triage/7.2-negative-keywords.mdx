# Negative Keywords: The Most Underused Profit Lever

Most Amazon advertisers obsess over adding keywords. Far fewer focus on removing them. Negative keywords — the search terms you explicitly tell Amazon NOT to show your ad for — are the simplest, most direct way to reduce wasted ad spend. A single well-chosen negative keyword can save hundreds of dollars per month with zero downside.

> 🎯 **Analogy**: Negative keywords are like a bouncer at the door of a nightclub. Without a bouncer, anyone can walk in — including people who will never buy a drink, cause problems, and take up space. A good bouncer keeps out the wrong crowd so the right crowd has a better experience. Your negative keywords keep out irrelevant shoppers so your budget goes toward the ones who actually convert.

## What Are Negative Keywords?

A negative keyword prevents your ad from showing for searches containing that term. When you add "cheap" as a negative keyword, Amazon will never show your ad for "cheap milk frother," "cheap kitchen gadgets," or any search with "cheap" in it.

**Negative exact match**: Blocks only the exact term (and close variants). Adding "cheap milk frother" as negative exact blocks that specific search but allows "cheap electric milk frother" or "best cheap milk frother."

**Negative phrase match**: Blocks any search containing the phrase. Adding "cheap" as negative phrase blocks every search with "cheap" anywhere in it.

> 📌 **Key Takeaway**: Phrase match gives broader protection but risks blocking relevant terms. Exact match is more precise but requires more entries. Choose based on whether you need a wide net or surgical precision.

## The Business Case for Negatives

Consider this scenario. You sell a premium milk frother at $39.99. Your broad match campaign targets "milk frother" and generates traffic for many search terms over 30 days:

| Search Term | Clicks | CPC | Spend | Orders | Sales | ACoS |
|-------------|--------|-----|-------|--------|-------|------|
| milk frother | 150 | $1.20 | $180 | 18 | $720 | 25% ✅ |
| electric milk frother | 80 | $1.10 | $88 | 12 | $480 | 18% ✅ |
| cheap milk frother | 45 | $0.90 | $41 | 0 | $0 | N/A ❌ |
| milk frother for kids | 30 | $1.00 | $30 | 0 | $0 | N/A ❌ |
| milk frother replacement parts | 25 | $1.35 | $34 | 0 | $0 | N/A ❌ |
| milk frother battery operated | 20 | $0.85 | $17 | 1 | $40 | 43% ❌ |

"cheap milk frother," "milk frother for kids," and "milk frother replacement parts" generated **$105 in spend with zero return**. Three negative keywords — "cheap," "kids," and "replacement parts" — immediately save $105/month. That's $1,260 per year of pure profit recovered with zero effort.

Now multiply this across all your campaigns. Negative keywords often reduce total spend by 15–25% with zero impact on sales — the very definition of free money.

> 💡 **Tip**: Negative keywords are the highest-ROI optimization you can make. They take 5 minutes to implement and save money every single day after.

## Building a Negative Keyword Strategy

### Step 1: Identify Irrelevant Patterns

Review your search term report and look for patterns of irrelevant traffic:

**Price-related**: "cheap," "budget," "inexpensive," "discount," "clearance" — if you sell premium products, these shoppers won't buy.

**Demographic mismatch**: "kids," "children," "toddler," "baby" — if your product isn't designed for children.

**Intent mismatch**: "repair," "replacement parts," "how to fix," "DIY" — these shoppers want to fix something, not buy new.

**Feature mismatch**: "battery operated," "manual," "hand crank" — if your product is electric and these shoppers want something different.

**Brand-specific**: "Nespresso," "Keurig," "Breville" — shoppers searching for a specific brand rarely switch.

### Step 2: Choose Match Type Strategically

Use **negative phrase** for broad patterns you want to eliminate entirely:
- "cheap" blocks all cheap-related searches
- "parts" blocks "replacement parts," "spare parts," "parts for [product]"

Use **negative exact** when you need precision:
- Specific irrelevant long-tail terms like "milk frother for toddlers"

**The cross-campaign negative**: If your exact match campaign targets "milk frother" and your broad match campaign also matches "milk frother" searches, add "milk frother" as a negative exact in the broad campaign. This prevents the broad campaign from competing with your exact campaign for the same search.

### Step 3: Implement at the Right Level

**Campaign-level negatives**: Apply to all ad groups. Use for terms universally irrelevant to your product.

**Ad group-level negatives**: Apply to a specific ad group only. Use when a term is irrelevant for one ad group but potentially relevant for another.

Most negatives should be campaign-level.

## Negative Keyword Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1: Over-negativing.** Adding too many negatives, especially phrase matches, can block relevant searches. "Manual" as a negative phrase blocks "manual milk frother" (irrelevant for electric) but also "instruction manual for electric milk frother" (a potential customer).

**Pitfall 2: Negativing without checking.** Before adding a negative, search Amazon for that term and verify your product wouldn't benefit from showing up.

**Pitfall 3: Ignoring exact match campaigns.** Exact match campaigns need fewer negatives, but Amazon's "close variants" can trigger ads for unintended terms. Still review search term reports.

**Pitfall 4: Not reviewing periodically.** Search trends change. A negative that made sense 6 months ago might be blocking valuable traffic today. Review quarterly.

**Pitfall 5: Using negatives instead of bid adjustments.** If a search term is relevant but expensive, don't negative it — lower your bid. Negatives are for irrelevant traffic, not expensive-but-relevant traffic.

> ⚠️ **Watch out**: Pitfall 5 is the most common strategic error. A term with 15% CVR but high CPC isn't irrelevant — it's just expensive. Lower the bid, don't kill the term.

## Negative Keyword Research Methods

Beyond your search term report, try these:

**Amazon search suggestions**: Type your main keyword into Amazon's search bar and note autocomplete suggestions. "Milk frother" suggests "milk frother for kids," "milk frother cheap" — potential negatives if irrelevant.

**Competitor product pages**: Check "Customers also bought" sections. Products from different categories represent negative keyword opportunities.

**Brand term analysis**: If your report shows clicks on competitor brands you can't compete with, add those brands as negatives.

## Try This: Build Your Negative List

You sell a $45 premium electric milk frother. Here are the search terms from this week's report that generated 5+ clicks with zero conversions:

1. "cheap milk frother" — 12 clicks, 0 orders
2. "milk frother for kids" — 8 clicks, 0 orders
3. "manual milk frother" — 7 clicks, 0 orders
4. "milk frother replacement parts" — 6 clicks, 0 orders
5. "nespresso milk frother" — 9 clicks, 0 orders
6. "milk frother recipe book" — 5 clicks, 0 orders

**Your task**: Decide the negative match type for each. Which get negative phrase and which get negative exact?

> 💡 **Suggested answers**: "cheap" (phrase), "kids" (phrase), "manual" (phrase — blocks "manual milk frother" and similar), "replacement parts" (phrase), "nespresso" (phrase), "recipe book" (phrase). All are patterns, not one-off terms, so phrase match gives broader protection.

## Measuring Impact

After adding negatives, compare before and after:

**Before negatives:**
- Spend: $500/week
- Sales: $1,500/week
- ACoS: 33%

**After negatives (4 weeks later):**
- Spend: $400/week (20% reduction)
- Sales: $1,450/week (3% decrease)
- ACoS: 28%

You saved $100/week while only losing $50 in sales — a net improvement of $50/week in profit and a 5-point ACoS improvement.

> 📌 **Key Takeaway**: Negative keywords are the simplest way to reduce wasted spend — often 15–25% reduction with zero sales impact. Use phrase for broad patterns, exact for precision. Review quarterly.
